The CRISPR Revolution

By Dr. Aiko Tanaka · March 12, 2026 · 9 minute read

Introduction

CRISPR is a family of DNA sequences found in the genomes of prokaryotic organisms. In the past decade it has been adapted as a precise tool for editing the genomes of living cells.

This article surveys the history of the technique, the major applications across medicine and agriculture, and the ethical questions that remain open.

A short history

The repeat sequences themselves were observed in 1987, but their function as part of an adaptive immune system in bacteria was not understood until the mid-2000s.

Early observations

Researchers at Osaka University reported the first repeated elements while studying the gene iap in E. coli.

The breakthrough

In 2012, Charpentier and Doudna demonstrated that the Cas9 enzyme could be programmed with a guide RNA to cleave DNA at a specified location.

Applications

  • Sickle cell disease therapies entered approved use in 2023.
  • Agricultural crops engineered for disease resistance.
  • Basic research into gene function across many species.

Open ethical questions

Germline editing — changes inherited by future generations — remains controversial and is prohibited in many jurisdictions.
